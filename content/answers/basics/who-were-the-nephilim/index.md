## Introduction

- The supernatural/angelic interpretation of the Nephilim (Genesis 6:1–4) has gained
  significant popular momentum in recent years
- This article does not aim to be definitive — the text is brief and the question
  is genuinely difficult
  - Only two explicit references to the Nephilim in all of Scripture
  - The primary passage is only a few verses (Genesis 6:1–4)
- Thesis: the textual, canonical, theological, and historical evidence leans toward
  a *human* genealogical interpretation — the "sons of God" as the faithful line of
  Seth, the "daughters of men" as the unfaithful line of Cain

## The Text: Genesis 6:1–4

- Read and briefly exposit the passage in full before arguing from it
- Identify the two competing interpretations:
  - **Supernatural view:** "sons of God" = angels or fallen angelic/demonic beings
    who mate with human women, producing hybrid offspring (the Nephilim)
  - **Genealogical/covenantal view:** "sons of God" = faithful line of Seth;
    "daughters of men" = unfaithful line of Cain; intermarriage leads to spiritual
    corruption and the wickedness that precipitates the Flood
- Note: this passage serves as the narrative bridge between the fall genealogies
  (Genesis 4–5) and the Flood (Genesis 6–9)

## Reason 1: The Context and Purpose of Genesis as a Book

- Genesis was authored by Moses for the people of Israel as they prepared to enter
  the Promised Land
  - The book is not merely abstract cosmic history — it speaks directly to Israel's
    historical situation
  - Genealogies, narratives, and figures in Genesis consistently relate to the
    nations and peoples surrounding Israel
- The recurring temptation throughout the entire Old Testament is Israel
  intermarrying with pagan peoples and being drawn into idolatry
  - This warning appears repeatedly: Deuteronomy 7, the period of the Judges,
    Solomon's foreign wives, Ezra/Nehemiah, etc.
  - The intermarriage interpretation of Genesis 6 fits this theme exactly
- The supernatural interpretation raises an obvious contextual problem:
  nowhere in the Old Testament is Israel warned against mating with demonic beings
  - What pastoral relevance would a story about angel-human hybrids have for the
    people entering Canaan?
  - The intermarriage warning, by contrast, is *immediately* relevant and pastorally
    urgent

## Reason 2: Genesis Is a Book of Human Genealogies — Two Rival Seeds

- From the moment of the Fall, Genesis establishes the framework of **two rival
  genealogical lines**
  - Genesis 3:15 — the *protoevangelium*: enmity between the seed of the serpent
    and the seed of the woman
  - These are not metaphors — they are literal human lines in conflict
- The division of humanity into two lines is traced through the early chapters:
  - **Line of Cain (Genesis 4):** marked by wickedness, culminating in Lamech —
    a figure of violence and pride ("I have killed a man for wounding me...
    Lamech seventy-sevenfold")
  - **Line of Seth (Genesis 4:25–5):** marked by faithfulness — "then men began
    to call on the name of the Lord"
- The parallel naming in the two genealogies is significant:
  - Both lines have an *Enoch* and a *Lamech* — but the characters are reversed
    moral mirrors of one another
  - Cain's Enoch founds a city (symbol of human pride); Seth's Enoch walks with
    God and is taken to heaven
  - Cain's Lamech boasts of murder; Seth's Lamech is the father of Noah
- Genesis 6 immediately follows Genesis 5 — the reader has just been walked
  through both genealogies
  - The most natural reading is that the author is *continuing* the two-seeds theme:
    the faithful line (sons of God) intermarries with the unfaithful line (daughters
    of men), and the result is catastrophic spiritual corruption
  - Introducing angels into this carefully constructed genealogical structure
    disrupts the entire literary logic of the book

## Reason 3: The Supernatural View Requires Unscriptural Assumptions About Angels

- Jesus teaches that in the resurrection, humans "neither marry nor are given in
  marriage" but are "like the angels" (Matthew 22:30)
  - The comparison implies that angels do not procreate — procreation is not part
    of angelic existence
- Angels in Scripture can appear in bodily form (e.g., the visitors to Abraham in
  Genesis 18), but they are not *embodied* beings in the way humans are
  - They were all created simultaneously — there is no angelic reproduction
- The angelic fall, while not described in extensive detail in Scripture, appears
  to have been a singular event — not an ongoing process of angels taking human wives
- For the supernatural view to work, one must posit:
  - Angels (or fallen angels/demons) capable of biological reproduction with humans
  - The existence of genuine human-angel hybrid offspring
  - None of this is supported anywhere else in Scripture
- It is methodologically problematic to derive a major theological claim about the
  nature of angels from one brief, obscure, disputed passage — especially when it
  contradicts the broader witness of Scripture

## Reason 4: The Supernatural View Depends Too Heavily on Second Temple Literature

- Much of the contemporary case for the supernatural interpretation leans on
  **pseudepigraphal literature**, especially the *Book of Enoch*
  - The *Book of Enoch* elaborates wildly on the Genesis 6 account, describing the
    "Watchers" (fallen angels) mating with women and producing the Nephilim
- Jude 14–15 does quote from 1 Enoch — but this does not grant the book
  quasi-canonical status
  - New Testament authors could cite extra-biblical sources to make a point without
    endorsing the entire document (cf. Paul citing Greek poets in Acts 17)
  - Jude's use of Enoch does not require acceptance of all Enochic traditions
- **The hermeneutical principle matters:** allowing second temple Jewish
  interpretive traditions to be *determinative* for reading the Old Testament
  leads to conclusions that frequently conflict with the New Testament's own
  handling of those texts
  - This is a principle worth examining carefully — where else does it lead?
- Second temple sources are valuable for understanding what *some Jews* thought
  at the time of the New Testament; they are not a reliable guide to what the Old
  Testament actually teaches

## Reason 5: The Supernatural View Is Not the Majority Position of the Church

- Among early Christian writers (pre-4th century), the supernatural interpretation
  does appear
  - It is not a modern invention — but neither is it as universally ancient as
    sometimes claimed
- **By the time of Augustine (late 4th century), the supernatural interpretation
  largely disappears** from prominent theological writing in both East and West
  - Augustine himself argues for the Sethite/genealogical interpretation
- The current revival of interest in the supernatural/Enochic interpretation
  appears to be largely a *recent* phenomenon
  - Much of it is being driven by popular-level content and, in some cases,
    Eastern Orthodox sources — though even the Eastern tradition historically
    did not hold this as a majority position
- **The weight of the argument:** departing from the historic consensus of the
  church requires strong exegetical and theological reasons
  - Reliance on second temple literature and the popularity of the view online
    do not constitute sufficient grounds

## Conclusion

- The Nephilim passage is genuinely brief and genuinely difficult — humility is
  appropriate on all sides
- But the convergence of evidence is significant:
  - Authorial context and purpose of Genesis point to the intermarriage
    interpretation
  - The two-seeds genealogical structure of Genesis 4–5 sets up Genesis 6 as
    its natural conclusion
  - Scripture elsewhere gives no warrant for angel-human reproduction
  - The case for the supernatural view rests significantly on non-canonical
    Jewish sources
  - The historic majority of the church read this passage the other way
- Practical/pastoral note: whatever one concludes about the Nephilim, the
  spiritual warning of the passage is clear — the corruption of the faithful
  through union with the unfaithful is catastrophic
  - This has immediate application for the life of the church today
